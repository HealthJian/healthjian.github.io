# Cherishing Summer and Saying Goodbye: Notes from Four Fleeting Years

June—the curtain of high summer has barely risen. This should be the season of flourishing, when life brims with its greatest vitality, yet the air always carries an indescribable melancholy—the distinctive scent of graduation and parting. The ancients often spoke of "autumn grief," but at this moment, I find myself seized by a profound "cherishing of summer." Just thinking about the once-bustling campus, the classmates with whom I shared daily life—all will soon vanish, everyone heading off to their own corners of the world. As someone naturally sentimental, my heart is filled with a heavy sense of loss. To keep these precious memories from scattering with the summer breeze, I've decided to type out this blog post, treating it as a solemn farewell and keepsake for this chapter of my youth.

My thoughts instantly drift back to the early autumn of 2022. Back then, my parents and I stepped together into this unfamiliar, distant city that I had pictured in my dreams countless times. It was our family's first long journey together, and facing the unknown, we were all at a loss. My parents learned to take the subway for the first time, stood in line for nucleic acid tests on strange streets for the first time, fumbled with their phone navigation for the first time... So many unexpected "firsts" broadened our horizons while also exposing our cognitive limits. Newly arrived, the weather was stiflingly hot; we barely sampled the local delicacies, only made a quick visit to the museum, and spent most of our time holed up in the hotel, exhausted. The most amusing yet bittersweet part: as northerners, we had no idea how to operate the central air conditioning without a remote control, and ended up enduring a sweltering night in over-30-degree heat. When we finally figured out how to switch to cooling the next day, it felt like a pardon from heaven—but soon, our stomachs protested from the chill, causing discomfort for quite a while. At that time, still clinging to high-school immaturity, I was baffled—and even resentful—of my parents' clumsy, timid behavior. Looking back now, I can only smile wryly and feel deep regret. Environment shapes character; labor refines it. If I could travel back, I wouldn't utter a single complaint, nor would I ever sulk at them. After two brief days in the city, I reported to school. Due to the pandemic restrictions at the time, my parents were stopped at the gate—the biggest regret of that summer. Watching their receding figures, I felt an emptiness in my heart, and countless times I fantasized about going home with them. But one must walk one's own path; growth, after all, is inevitable.

The start of college life was accompanied by a deep sense of "unwillingness." Like almost all freshmen around me, everyone seemed to bear some lingering dissatisfaction with their Gaokao results, harboring a fierce drive to prove themselves anew. My first four dormmates got along very well (one later transferred out, and a veteran returned from military service to join us); we respected each other, and even after we were reassigned to different rooms, we stayed in touch. But early on, I was completely oblivious to the cutthroat "involution" that was about to engulf everything. When I was still feeling proud of scoring 90 in a course, I was shocked to find that others were deeply anxious because they had "only" scored 99. That was my wake-up call: I had grossly underestimated the competition at university. So I, too, was dragged into the frenzy—grabbing seats in study lounges, forcing myself to grind. Yet the more I conformed, the more I resisted. This was not the college life I wanted. Gradually, I started slacking off. Later, campus protests erupted over lockdown policies, leading to an early break and postponed final exams. That snapped the taut string entirely; I completely let go, sinking into extreme idleness and indiscipline. When make-up exams came after winter break, I barely scraped by with low scores across the board, and my overall GPA for freshman year took a heavy hit. Though it's never too late to mend, and I worked desperately to pull my grades up in the second semester, I still gravely underestimated the brutality of this competition. Can you imagine? A classmate actually pushed themselves to study over ten hours daily, to the point of developing heart problems and being forced to take a medical leave. All of it suffocated me.

After changing dormmates, I transferred into the Data Science department. Faced with even more rigorous coursework, I grew more lost, tense, and anxious. Conversations around me about graduate school recommendations and big-tech internships felt simultaneously near and far. My grades hovered in a middling range, stubbornly stuck, and I found myself on the precarious edge of our school's internal postgraduate recommendation threshold. From sophomore year onward, I felt like I was soaked in bitter anxiety, with barely a moment to breathe. Watching friends and classmates shine so brightly that it hurt my eyes, I grew increasingly pessimistic about my own future compared to theirs. Sophomore year was without a doubt the hardest of my academic life—packed with difficult, heavy major courses, all while my mindset was unusually restless, leading to unsatisfactory grades in several core subjects. I could never shake off the grip of "meritocracy," and university happens to be a small society where "GPA reigns supreme." Under that evaluation system, I stumbled at every turn, constrained and frustrated. By junior year, I worked like a madman, earning near-perfect grades in almost all courses, yet still couldn't fully erase the impact of those few "once-and-for-all" low scores from freshman and sophomore years. My overall rank remained precarious. Plus, my single-minded focus on further studies made me miss the optimal window for internships. So I began preparing for the postgraduate entrance exam while keeping an eye on recommendation dynamics. But fate loves to play tricks: the academic affairs office suddenly changed its admissions policy, implementing a "domestic-first" rule for the recommendation list, followed by a sharp cut in the number of internal recommendation slots. That notice dashed all my hopes, leaving me utterly disheartened.

Looking back on the grad school exam experience now still stirs a mix of emotions. That semester I had a heavy course load and could only prepare for the exam in the gaps between major classes. Watching classmates around me claim their successes—securing spots at elite "C9" universities—became a familiar refrain, adding another layer of crushing pressure. But luckily, I still retained a streak of optimism, which alleviated the stress and kept me feeling relatively fine, without overly disrupting my life and studies. My school selection process was also peculiar: I first set my sights on Xi'an Jiaotong and Renmin University's Suzhou campus, but somehow later switched to Fudan. In hindsight, this year the former two turned out to be notably easier to get into, while Fudan saw an unprecedented surge in competitiveness. And so, my exam attempt ended in failure in an unexpected way. Fortunately, after learning of my rejection, my parents didn't put any pressure on me—not a single reproach. I know they must have felt disappointed, regretful, even resentful inside, but they absorbed all those negative emotions themselves, never passing any negativity to me. For that, I am immensely grateful.

After the exam failure, I dared not face a second attempt. Throwing myself into the spring recruitment frenzy, I found that road equally tough. Dozens of resumes sent out vanished without a trace. In stark contrast, those who had secured grad school offers or early internships—whether continuing to intern or job-hunting—seemed to sail smoothly, which multiplied my stress. The shaping power of environment bore down on me mercilessly, plunging me deep into anxiety and confusion. A string of failed interviews left me somewhat pessimistic, and even my parents' patient reassurances couldn't fully soothe me. The twin mountains of "insufficient credentials" and "lack of experience" weighed heavily on my back, leaving me trapped in an awkward middle ground—neither here nor there—and exceptionally uncomfortable.

But turning points often come at the darkest hour. After a long, agonizing wait, I finally received offers from three companies and ultimately chose a decent job. Though the starting salary is low—perhaps less than a fifth of what some peers earn—I am content with it for now. The moment I signed the tripartite agreement, a thought struck me: had I not genuinely dived into hardcore projects like industrial image classification and end-cloud collaborative system architecture during my undergrad years, had I not quietly accumulated those foundational skills, I probably wouldn't have even landed this outcome under the grilling of multiple interviews. It turns out that the detours I took and the sweat I shed all count in the end.

One must learn to be content. Though I still feel utterly uncertain about the future, at least now I have a solid starting point beneath my feet. Now it's time to graduate. After four years, this once-strange city has become intimately familiar, and I've discovered many hidden culinary gems in its alleys. But this time, my parents cannot come—they are tied up with work and cannot leave their posts, which is another great regret. My classmates and friends are also about to scatter; their prospects are brighter than mine, they soar higher, and many have achieved both love and career success. While full of envy, I sincerely wish them all the best.

The June wind carries summer's blaze and the sigh of farewell. Writing this long piece feels like reliving the ups and downs of these four years all over again—from the greenness and bewilderment of freshman entry, to the confusion and struggle of being swept into involution; from the darkest hour of grad school failure, to the relief of landing a job in spring recruitment. Through all these trials and transformations, I have finally carved out a shape that is my own. The vibrant summer belongs not only to the luxuriance of trees, but also to our ripening beyond naivety. After the brief disappointment, the road ahead still beckons. Farewell, my university; farewell, this city full of laughter and tears. With gratitude and regret, I will pack my bags and calmly set out for the next horizon. May we all fare well in the years when we cannot see each other.
