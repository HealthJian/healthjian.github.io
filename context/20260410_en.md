# One Incident Exposed My Ignorance, Impatience, and Anxiety

Ever since the 2026 graduate entrance exam results were finalized, that outcome symbolizing "failure" became a watershed in my life. My mindset went through violent deconstruction and reconstruction: from the calm and confidence I had during exam preparation, to the late-night spiral of reflection and inferiority, and finally to putting on a mask to hide my inner confusion. After cleaning up the aftermath of the exam, I hurried into the spring recruitment wave, only to crash into another brutal battle where millions compete for very few chances. In this campaign, I was once lucky enough to get an unusually smooth opportunity, which made me mistakenly believe that I had finally met a benefactor and that fate had begun to favor me. Yet the turning point came without warning. Between extreme highs and lows, my psychological defenses were completely shattered. After painful reflection, this late confession came into being.

For someone like me, who missed autumn recruitment because of the exam and had almost no meaningful offline internship or job-hunting experience, spring recruitment felt unfamiliar and cold. Its cycle is extremely short. People in the workplace always say "Golden March, Silver April," and now April is already half gone, while I still drift like a leaf in the wind. I submitted resumes to more than thirty companies. Except for those I strategically gave up because they were too far away, or those I could not attend due to schedule conflicts, most of the remaining resumes sank into the sea, stuck in long and tedious processes with no response.

Just when I was close to despair, a "lifeline" appeared. In that interview, I felt as if all my channels suddenly opened, and I delivered an almost perfect presentation. I not only impressed the technical manager and won his strong approval, but I was even moved by my own passion. The following HR round progressed unusually smoothly. We even had a pleasant exchange and discussed salary and benefits in detail. At that moment, I was overwhelmed by joy and my confidence inflated rapidly. What was a failed exam in comparison? I felt almost triumphant, as if I had won everything in one battle. I thought victory had come so easily that I began to overlook everything else, and my mindset became overheated.

Yet the dramatic ups and downs of life often happen in an instant. The process stopped abruptly. Overnight, I realized I had nothing except a lightweight "verbal candidate intention." For this so-called "deep communication," I even turned down an important written test from another company. At the same time, other application processes also seemed frozen for days and weeks without any feedback. I fell from the clouds into an abyss. The shadow of defeatism quickly swallowed the faint light of optimism. That entire day, I felt like a deflated balloon, exhausted and dispirited, relying only on forced smiles to pretend everything was fine.

From intense joy to sorrow, from sorrow back to joy, then from joy to sorrow again - this severe emotional oscillation mercilessly pulled at my nerves and exposed my personality flaws. At my core, I am a contradictory blend of emotion and reason: anxious about all these changes at night, yet forcing myself every morning to believe that "everything will get better." The root cause of this unstable mentality is my ignorance of how society actually works and my lack of preparedness for setbacks.

In life, eight or nine things out of ten go against our wishes. Difficulties and setbacks are the norm; smooth sailing is the exception. But no matter how sad I am, the sun still rises, life still moves, and reality never pauses because of my personal will. Looking back, my recent "joy" came from grabbing a lifeline and mistakenly believing I could pick the fruit without enough patience and accumulation. At that moment, arrogance, superiority, and ignorance occupied the high ground of my mind. My current "sorrow," on the other hand, came after setbacks made me overly fearful, mistakenly believing I had completely failed, that all previous effort was wasted, and then letting frustration and worry overrun my reason.

This emotional volatility is deeply wrong. It is the concrete expression of my ignorance and impatience. Corporate approval processes will not speed up because I feel upset, and life’s deadlocks will not resolve themselves because I overthink. The only thing I can do is admit mistakes, review, restart, and truly suppress my restlessness. People around me once described me as "honest, kind, and steady." But after this round of setbacks, I realized I seemed to have changed - becoming utilitarian, impatient, and consumed by gains and losses. This must be corrected immediately.

After being tempered by this experience, I finally saw the truth behind my repeated emotional swings: I was dragged too hastily by this flood called "job hunting." Ignorance, urgency, and anxiety completely disrupted my pace and made me suffer needlessly under defeatism.

But what is this, really? I am only at the very beginning of life. I still have time, and I must make every day count. Of course, the premise is self-reconstruction: I need to set my mindset right, correct my understanding of society, and realign my path for the future.

As I write these words now, optimism inside me has quietly revived. Letting go of restlessness and facing my ignorance honestly, I will still get ready again and continue walking this road full of uncertainty and challenges - tirelessly, and without stopping the fight.
