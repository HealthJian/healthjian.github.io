## 1. What Is an Eco-Aquarium? – Core Principles

An eco‑aquarium is essentially **a miniature natural ecosystem simulated inside a glass box**. It consists of three components: plants (producers), fish and shrimp (consumers), and bacteria (decomposers). Together they form a closed loop of material cycling – **fish waste → microbes break it down → plants absorb nutrients → oxygen is released** – allowing for low‑maintenance or even "no‑water‑change" operation.

### The Nitrogen Cycle: The Lifeline of Your Eco‑Tank

The nitrogen cycle is the single most critical process for your fish’s survival. A simplified model looks like this:

**Fish waste / leftover food → Ammonia (highly toxic) → Nitrite (highly toxic) → Nitrate (low toxicity) → Absorbed by plants**

Fish excrement and uneaten food are decomposed by microbes into **ammonia**. **Nitrosomonas bacteria** then convert ammonia into **nitrite**, and **Nitrobacter bacteria** further convert nitrite into **nitrate**. **Both ammonia and nitrite are highly toxic to fish.** Nitrate is less toxic, but high concentrations can still harm fish. The core task of an eco‑aquarium is to keep this chain running smoothly – nitrifying bacteria handle the toxins, and plants absorb the final product (nitrate).

## 2. Pre‑Setup Preparation: Supply List

For a **60 cm tank**, here’s the essential equipment:

| Category | Recommended Choice | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------|
| **Container** | Ultra‑clear glass tank (60×30×35 cm) or ceramic pot | Recommended size ≥40 cm; avoid round bowls |
| **Substrate** | Lava rock (3‑5 mm) + river sand / stream gravel | Total thickness 5‑8 cm; lava rock is porous and good for bacteria |
| **Plants** | Fast‑growing species (green cabomba, hornwort) + floating plants | Fast‑growers should make up >60% of the plant mass |
| **Livestock** | Ricefish / White Cloud Mountain minnows + Cherry shrimp + Ramshorn snails | Total fish length < tank length / 10 |
| **Lighting** | Full‑spectrum LED aquarium light | Colour temperature 6000‑8000K, 6‑8 hours daily |
| **Tools** | Tweezers, siphon, water test kits | Ammonia test kit is essential in the early stage |

**Substrate layering order**: bottom layer – 3‑5 cm of large lava rock → middle layer – small lava rock → top layer – river sand. This layered structure ensures good aeration and prevents dust from clouding the water.

## 3. Seven‑Step Tank Setup Process
![开缸七步法](../images/blog/开缸七步法.avif)
### Step 1: Cleaning and Disinfection

Rinse the tank and lava rock thoroughly with clean water. You can soak the rocks in a potassium permanganate solution (deep purple colour) for disinfection, then rinse repeatedly with clean water. **Never use dish soap** – residual chemicals will kill your fish.

### Step 2: Lay the Substrate

Follow the order: large rock at the bottom → small rock in the middle → river sand on top. Create a **slope from back to front** – this not only adds visual depth but also helps water circulation.

### Step 3: Plant the Aquarium Plants

Before planting, soak the plants in a **mild salt solution** (3‑5 g salt per 100 ml water) for 10 minutes, rinse them off, and trim any dead or damaged leaves.

Layout principle – **layer your plants** for a three‑dimensional landscape:

- **Background**: tall, fast‑growing stem plants (green cabomba, hornwort), planted 5 cm apart and deeply rooted
- **Midground**: ferns, moss (tied to driftwood)
- **Foreground**: low‑growing plants or open sand
- **Water surface**: floating plants covering about 30% of the surface – they shade out algae and absorb ammonia efficiently

### Step 4: Fill the Tank Slowly

Place a plate or bowl on the substrate and **pour the new water gently against the bowl** to avoid disturbing the substrate and uprooting plants. **Tap water must be left to stand for 24 hours to remove chlorine.**

### Step 5: Cycling the Water – Patience Is Key

**This is where most beginners fail.** The nitrification system takes time to establish – **at least 15‑30 days**.

In the early stage, **do not add any livestock**. Run the light 4‑6 hours a day. The water will go through a “**clear → cloudy → clear again**” phase – cloudiness is a normal sign that nitrifying bacteria are multiplying. **Don’t do a water change too soon.**

When you see green algae appearing on the glass, it indicates the nitrogen cycle is progressing. **Ammonia and nitrite readings at zero** are the real signs that the system is mature.
![开缸养水“清-浊-清”时序演变图](../images/blog/开缸养水“清-浊-清”时序演变图.avif)
### Step 6: Introduce “Cycle” Fish

Start with 1‑2 hardy fish (e.g., zebrafish) or a few snails, and observe them for 5‑7 days. If they are active and the water stays clear, gradually add more livestock.

**Key tip for introducing fish/shrimp**: Float the bag with fish in the tank for **30 minutes to equalise temperature**, then gradually add small amounts of tank water to the bag – this is called “temperature and water acclimation” and prevents sudden changes that can kill fish.

### Step 7: Add Your Main Fish – Keep Density Strict

**Golden rule: 1 cm of fish per litre of water.** For example, a 60 cm tank holds about 100 litres, so at most 10 fish of 5 cm each. **Less is always better** – too many fish will crash the ecosystem.


## 4. Aquascaping Aesthetics: Making Your Tank a Living Painting

An eco‑aquarium is not just science – it’s also art.

### Composition Principles

Use the **rule of thirds or golden ratio** to divide the layout. Place the main focal point (driftwood, rocks) at the golden‑ratio intersection for a naturally comfortable visual balance.

### Plant Colour Combinations

- **Greens**: moss, water wisteria – as a base layer
- **Reds**: red ludwigia – for accents, requires stronger light
- **Surface**: floating plants (duckweed, water lentils) – add depth

### Hardscape Selection

Driftwood releases tannins that help stabilise water quality; dragon stone or seiryu stone adds texture. **Make sure rocks are stable** – fish or shrimp can burrow underneath and cause collapse.


## 5. Choosing Livestock: Who Will Thrive in Your Underwater World?

### Beginner‑Friendly Fish

| Species | Characteristics | Suitable Temperature |
|---------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Ricefish** | Small (3‑5 cm), tolerates 0‑30°C, low oxygen demand | 0‑30°C |
| **White Cloud Mountain Minnow** | Wide temperature range, will clean algae | 5‑30°C |
| **Zebrafish** | Extremely hardy, great for cycling | 18‑26°C |
| **Guppy** | Colourful, breeds easily | 22‑28°C |

### Clean‑Up Crew

- **Cherry shrimp**: excellent algae eaters, density 1‑2 per litre
- **Ramshorn snails**: clean leftover food and substrate, 1‑2 per 10 litres
- **Corydoras catfish**: turn over the substrate to prevent compaction

⚠️ **Avoid**: goldfish and koi – they produce too much waste for a small eco‑tank.


## 6. Daily Maintenance: Observe More, Tinker Less

The beauty of an eco‑aquarium lies in **long‑term fine‑tuning**, not dramatic water changes.

### Feeding

- **1‑2 times a day**, only as much as they can eat in 3 minutes
- **Less is more** – fish die more often from overfeeding (and the resulting pollution) than from hunger
- Skip feeding one day a week to let the system clean up leftover nutrients

### Water Changes and Top‑Ups

- For evaporation, just **top up with dechlorinated water**.
- If a water change is needed, **change 1/4‑1/3 weekly**, keeping the new water temperature within ±2°C of the tank.
- **Avoid large water changes** – they wash away the beneficial microbial community.

### Plant Trimming

Trim fast‑growing floating plants and hornwort regularly. **Trimming is essentially removing nitrogen from the system** – plants absorb nitrate and grow; when you cut and discard the excess growth, you export that nitrogen out of the tank.


## 7. Common Problems and Solutions

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **Cloudy water** | Nitrification not established, or livestock added too early | Stop feeding, let it sit for 7‑10 days to clear naturally |
| **Algae bloom** | Too much light or excess nutrients | Reduce lighting, increase floating plant cover, introduce cherry shrimp |
| **Yellowing plants** | Old leaves shedding or insufficient light | Trim dead leaves; new shoots will grow in time |
| **Fish gasping at surface** | Oxygen deficiency or ammonia poisoning | Check density, reduce bioload, do a small water change if necessary |


## Final Thoughts

The eco‑aquarium has taught me far more than just how to keep fish. It reminded me of a simple truth: **the stability of any system comes from balance** – not too many fish, not too few plants; not too much light, nor too little.

**Slow down, and give the system time.** Nitrifying bacteria need a month to settle; plants need time to root; the whole ecosystem needs patience to reach equilibrium. Many beginners fail not because they did something wrong, but because they **rushed** – they added fish on day 7 when the water looked clear, unaware that it was the peak of nitrite concentration.

Raise the water first, then raise the fish – and in doing so, raise your own patience. May you find peace and healing in this little underwater world of yours. 🐠
