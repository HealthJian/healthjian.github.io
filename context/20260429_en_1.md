## 1. Background: Why Does Bearing Fault Diagnosis Need Deep Learning?

### 1.1 Core Pain Points in Industrial Scenarios

Transmission devices are among the most commonly used mechanical equipment in daily life and industrial production. **Bearings**, as vulnerable components, directly impact the safe operation of the entire system. Under different working conditions, bearing status can change rapidly and unpredictably, making **early fault diagnosis** crucial for preventing safety hazards.

In the era of big data, the continuous upgrade of detection and data recording methods has brought urgent demands for machine learning in industrial fault diagnosis:

| Traditional Method | Limitations |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Wavelet Transform / EMD | Can diagnose but with limited accuracy, lacking interpretability |
| Traditional CNN | Can learn from datasets but insufficient feature extraction |
| Single Swarm Optimization (e.g., AFSA) | Better than random signal separation, but prone to local optima |

### 1.2 Three Major Deficiencies in Existing Methods

The paper explicitly identifies the core problems in current bearing fault diagnosis:

1. **Insufficient accuracy**: Classification precision of traditional models needs improvement
2. **Weak interpretability**: The diagnostic decision process is a "black box" — unable to explain to engineers "why it was diagnosed as a fault"
3. **Poor robustness**: Single optimization algorithms lack effective parameter selection methods, easily leading to sample dependency, initial weight sensitivity, and slow learning efficiency

### 1.3 The Paper's Core Approach

To address these issues, the paper proposes an end-to-end solution:

$$\text{Raw Vibration Signal} \xrightarrow{\text{2D Wavelet}} \text{Time-Frequency Image} \xrightarrow{\text{GWO-AlexNet}} \text{Fault Classification} \xrightarrow{\text{Grad-CAM}} \text{Decision Explanation}$$

The **core innovation** lies in: combining swarm optimization (GWO) with deep learning (AlexNet), while introducing Grad-CAM for diagnostic interpretability.

## 2. Methodology: Full Technical Architecture

### 2.1 AlexNet Network Architecture

AlexNet consists of 5 convolutional layers and 3 fully connected layers, with an input size of $227 \times 227 \times 3$. Key design features include:

- **ReLU activation**: Replaces Sigmoid, effectively solving the vanishing gradient problem in deeper networks and accelerating training
- **Overlapping pooling**: Pooling stride smaller than kernel size, enriching feature representation and enhancing recognition performance
- **Dropout regularization**: Applied in fully connected layers to prevent overfitting

```
Input (227×227×3)
  → Conv1 → ReLU → Norm → Pool1 (55×55×96)
  → Conv2 → ReLU → Norm → Pool2 (27×27×256)
  → Conv3 → ReLU (13×13×384)
  → Conv4 → ReLU (13×13×256)
  → Conv5 → ReLU → Pool5 (6×6×256)
  → FC6 → ReLU → Dropout (4096)
  → FC7 → ReLU → Dropout (4096)
  → FC8 → Softmax (4-class output)
```

### 2.2 t-SNE Dimensionality Reduction Visualization

t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding) is a nonlinear dimensionality reduction algorithm used to visualize data distribution in high-dimensional feature spaces. Its core idea proceeds as follows:

**Step 1**: In high-dimensional space, represent pairwise similarity using conditional probability:

$$P_{j|i} = \frac{\exp(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2 / 2\sigma_i^2)}{\sum_{k \neq i} \exp(-\|x_i - x_k\|^2 / 2\sigma_i^2)}$$

**Step 2**: Symmetrize the joint probability distribution:

$$P_{ij} = \frac{P_{j|i} + P_{i|j}}{2}$$

**Step 3**: In low-dimensional space, compute similarity using the t-distribution:

$$q_{ij} = \frac{(1 + \|y_i - y_j\|^2)^{-1}}{\sum_{k \neq l}(1 + \|y_k - y_l\|^2)^{-1}}$$

**Step 4**: Minimize KL divergence via gradient descent to update the low-dimensional representation:

$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial y_i} = 4 \sum_j (P_{ij} - q_{ij})(y_i - y_j)(1 + \|y_i - y_j\|^2)^{-1}$$

In this paper, t-SNE is used to visualize AlexNet's feature extraction effectiveness at different learning rates — the tighter same-class samples cluster and the greater the separation between different classes, the more effective the model's feature extraction.

### 2.3 Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO)

GWO is a swarm intelligence optimization algorithm inspired by the social hierarchy and hunting behavior of grey wolves. The pack hierarchy is:

```
α (Alpha)  — Best solution
β (Beta)   — Second-best solution
δ (Delta)  — Third-best solution
ω (Omega)  — Followers
```

**Mathematical Modeling of the Hunting Process**:

Distance formula and position update between grey wolf and prey:

$$D = |C \cdot X_p(t) - X(t)|$$
$$X(t+1) = X_p(t) - A \cdot D$$

Where the coefficient vectors are:

$$A = 2a \cdot r_1 - a, \quad C = 2 \cdot r_2$$

$a$ linearly decreases from 2 to 0 (balancing exploration and exploitation), $r_1, r_2$ are random vectors in $[0,1]$.

**Position Tracking Model**: Under the guidance of α, β, and δ wolves, individual positions are updated:

$$X_{t+1} = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + X_3}{3}$$

Where $X_1, X_2, X_3$ are derived from the positions of α, β, and δ respectively.

**Application in this paper**: GWO jointly optimizes two key hyperparameters of AlexNet — **Batch Size** and **Learning Rate** — with the highest test accuracy as the optimization objective.

### 2.4 Grad-CAM: Decision Interpretability

Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping) is a visual interpretation technique for deep learning. It uses gradient information to compute the activation map of a CNN for the input image, revealing which regions most influence the classification decision.

**Weight Computation**:

$$\alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z} \sum_i \sum_j \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A_{ij}^k}$$

Where $\alpha_k^c$ is the weight of the $k$-th feature map for class $c$, $A_{ij}^k$ is the pixel value at coordinate $(i,j)$ in the $k$-th feature map, and $y^c$ is the score for class $c$.

**Generating the Heatmap**: All feature maps are weighted and summed, then passed through ReLU to filter out negative values, producing the final class activation heatmap. Redder regions indicate higher network attention, meaning those regions contribute more to the fault determination.

## 3. Experimental Pipeline: Five-Step Methodology

The paper designs a complete five-step experimental pipeline:

```
Step 1: 2D Wavelet Transform → Generate time-frequency signal images
    ↓
Step 2: Input to AlexNet & GoogLeNet → Noise immunity comparison
    ↓
Step 3: t-SNE visualization → Evaluate feature separation at different learning rates
    ↓
Step 4: Swarm optimization comparison (CS/BOA/GWO/WOA) → Find optimal algorithm
    ↓
Step 5: Grad-CAM → Generate decision explanation heatmaps
```

### 3.1 Dataset

The experimental data comes from the **Case Western Reserve University (CWRU) Bearing Dataset**, the most classic public benchmark in bearing fault diagnosis.

| Condition | Training Samples | Test Samples |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Normal | 30 | 70 |
| Inner Race Fault | 30 | 70 |
| Rolling Element Fault | 30 | 70 |
| Outer Race Fault | 30 | 70 |
| **Total** | **120** | **280** |

Experimental conditions: fault diameter 0.014 inches, drive rotation 1797 r/min.

### 3.2 Data Preprocessing

Raw one-dimensional vibration signals are converted to $227 \times 227$ time-frequency energy maps through **2D wavelet transform**:

- Wavelet transform captures both time-domain and frequency-domain information simultaneously, reflecting vibration frequency response characteristics caused by faults
- Converted images are cropped to AlexNet's standard input format $227 \times 227 \times 3$
- Time-frequency images of four different states (Normal/Inner/Rolling/Outer) exhibit visually distinct texture patterns

### 3.3 Noise Immunity Comparison

The paper compares the noise immunity performance of AlexNet and GoogLeNet at a signal-to-noise ratio of 15dB. Results show that **AlexNet outperforms GoogLeNet in handling noise interference**.

### 3.4 Swarm Optimization Algorithm Comparison

The paper compares four mainstream swarm optimization algorithms, using accuracy and runtime as evaluation metrics:

| Algorithm | Runtime (sec) | Accuracy | Efficiency |
|-----------|--------------|----------|------------|
| CS (Cuckoo Search) | 4632.8 | 97.50% | 2.10 |
| BOA (Butterfly Optimization) | 7314.3 | 98.33% | 1.34 |
| **GWO (Grey Wolf Optimizer)** | **1307.6** | **99.17%** | **7.58** |
| WOA (Whale Optimization) | 12426 | 97.50% | 0.78 |

GWO achieves the best performance in both runtime and accuracy — its runtime is only 1/10 of WOA while achieving the highest accuracy of 99.17%.

### 3.5 Grad-CAM Decision Explanation

The heatmaps generated by Grad-CAM reveal:

- **Normal state**: Network attention is evenly distributed, with no abnormally high-attention regions
- **Inner race fault**: Network focuses on specific frequency regions corresponding to characteristic vibration frequencies caused by inner race damage
- **Rolling element fault**: Attention regions align with rolling element fault frequency characteristics
- **Outer race fault**: High-attention areas clearly point to outer race fault characteristic frequency regions

Redder colors indicate higher network attention, meaning those parts contribute more to the fault probability determination. This provides engineers with intuitive visual evidence to understand and trust the model's diagnostic decisions.

## 4. Innovation Summary

### 4.1 Signal-to-Image Processing

Converting one-dimensional vibration signals to time-frequency image signals through 2D wavelet transform significantly reduces the complexity of signal feature extraction while fully leveraging CNN's advantages in image classification.

### 4.2 Transfer Learning + Swarm Optimization

Retaining the pre-trained AlexNet's shallow structure and weights (for universal low-level feature extraction) while fine-tuning only the higher-level structure, combined with GWO hyperparameter optimization, achieves 99.17% diagnostic accuracy under small-sample conditions (only 120 training samples).

### 4.3 Decision Interpretability

By using Grad-CAM to visualize the "black box" model's diagnostic decisions as heatmaps, the diagnostic results gain physically meaningful interpretation for engineers — not only telling you "there is a fault" but also showing "what the model based its judgment on."

## 5. Limitations and Extended Reflections

### 5.1 Dataset Scale

While the CWRU dataset is a classic benchmark, its sample size is relatively small (only 100 samples per class). In real industrial scenarios, data volumes are typically much larger and working conditions more complex. The model's generalization ability needs further verification.

### 5.2 Fault Type Coverage

The paper only considers four states (normal + three single faults), without addressing compound faults (e.g., simultaneous inner race + outer race failure), which can occur in real-world conditions.

### 5.3 Real-time Performance

The GWO optimization process takes 1307.6 seconds (approximately 22 minutes). While far superior to WOA, for industrial scenarios requiring real-time online diagnosis, can this training time be further compressed?

### 5.4 Model Lightweighting

While AlexNet was groundbreaking in its time (2012), it appears "heavy" by today's standards. Could more lightweight networks (such as MobileNet or EfficientNet) serve as replacements, maintaining accuracy while reducing deployment costs?

### 5.5 More Advanced Interpretability Techniques

While Grad-CAM is intuitive and effective, more refined interpretability methods have emerged in recent years, such as Score-CAM (gradient-free), Attention Rollout (for Transformers), and SHAP (model-agnostic). Introducing these techniques to bearing fault diagnosis could further enhance explanation quality.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper proposes a complete end-to-end bearing fault diagnosis pipeline: "Signal Preprocessing → Deep Learning Classification → Interpretable Decision Making." Its core contributions are:

1. **Methodological completeness**: From raw signals to final interpretable diagnostic results, forming a closed loop
2. **GWO optimization effectiveness**: Standing out among four swarm optimization algorithms, achieving the highest accuracy in the shortest time
3. **Engineering value of interpretability**: Grad-CAM heatmaps endow diagnostic results with physical meaning, enhancing model trustworthiness

For engineers and researchers working in intelligent manufacturing, Industrial IoT, and predictive maintenance, this paper offers a valuable methodological framework — organically combining deep learning's "perception capability," swarm intelligence's "optimization capability," and explainable AI's "transparency" to provide a viable technical pathway for industrial-grade fault diagnosis.
