# HR Interview Preparation Notes and Practical Guide

This note is for campus and experienced candidates. The goal is to help you ask the right questions in HR interviews while staying professional, polite, and tactful.

---

## 1. Clarify your objective

An HR interview is not just about asking for money. It is about confirming collaboration terms:

- Verify the real job conditions (pay, schedule, location, contract, growth).
- Evaluate fit (team style, stability, career path).
- Build clear, confirmable agreements (ideally in writing).

Mindset:

> Ask to confirm facts and avoid misunderstandings, not to challenge or confront.

---

## 2. Compensation structure and payout timing

You should clarify:

- Base salary (monthly/annual)
- Bonus (performance, annual bonus, project bonus, sign-on)
- Allowances (meal, housing, transport, phone, travel, etc.)
- Benefits (commercial insurance, checkups, holiday package)
- Social insurance + housing fund (or extra insurance): city, base, ratio, effective date
- Payroll date and whether payment is delayed

### Suggested order

1. Total package structure (fixed vs variable)
2. Fixed salary payout rule
3. Variable payout conditions and timing
4. Insurance/fund city, base, ratio
5. Pre-tax vs post-tax understanding

### Tactful phrasing

- “To avoid any misunderstanding on my side, may I confirm the fixed vs variable ratio in this package?”
- “Is base paid by calendar month or attendance cycle? What is the regular payroll date?”
- “For social insurance and housing fund, is it paid in the work-location city? How is the base determined?”
- “How is bonus usually linked to performance, and what is the historical payout rhythm?”

### What to record

- Total pre-tax package, pre-tax base, variable ratio
- Payroll date, annual bonus range
- Probation salary ratio
- Insurance/fund city and ratio (company/employee)

---

## 3. Work location, meals, housing, and rent support

Confirm:

- Primary work location (fixed vs multi-site/assignment)
- Office type (HQ/factory/client site)
- Meals and accommodation policy (dorm, meal allowance, standards)
- Commute support (shuttle, parking, transport subsidy)
- Relocation support for cross-city onboarding

Tactful phrasing:

- “To plan commuting and housing in advance, may I confirm whether the role is stably based in XX?”
- “Do you provide dorm or meal allowance or shuttle support?”
- “For cross-city onboarding, is there relocation or temporary accommodation support?”

---

## 4. Headcount type, contract, and tripartite agreement

Core checks:

- Direct labor contract with company entity
- Legal entity signing the contract (parent/subsidiary/vendor)
- Formal employee headcount vs outsourced/dispatch model
- Campus hiring: tripartite agreement timeline and terms

Tactful phrasing:

- “To ensure smooth process on my side, may I confirm which legal entity signs the labor contract?”
- “Is this role formal headcount, or does it involve outsourcing or dispatch?”
- “For campus hiring, could you share the tripartite agreement timeline and key terms in advance?”

---

## 5. Probation: duration, salary, KPI

Must-ask items:

- Probation length (commonly 1-6 months)
- Probation salary ratio (commonly 80%-100%)
- Conversion criteria (KPI/OKR, project milestones, capability review)
- Reviewer and review cycle

Tactful phrasing:

- “I would like to ramp up quickly. Could you share the key probation evaluation criteria?”
- “Is probation pay a fixed ratio, or adjusted by evaluation outcome?”
- “When does conversion review usually start? Is early conversion possible when goals are met earlier?”

---

## 6. Work schedule, overtime, shift pattern

Confirm:

- Workweek model (two-day weekend, alternating weekends, single day off)
- Standard hours and clock-in rules
- Overtime frequency and peak periods
- Overtime compensation or comp-off policy
- Shift requirement (day/night rotation, cycle, subsidies)

Tactful phrasing:

- “To better match the team rhythm, may I understand the normal schedule and peak-season arrangement?”
- “During project sprints, is compensation mainly comp-off, or is there a defined overtime payment policy?”
- “Does this role require shifts? If yes, what is the rotation cycle and allowance rule?”

---

## 7. Growth and promotion path

Ask about:

- Dual track (management vs specialist)
- Promotion cadence (semi-annual/annual)
- Capability model and expected outcomes
- Mentorship, training budget, rotation opportunities

Tactful phrasing:

- “I care about long-term growth. What is the typical 1-2 year development path for this role?”
- “Do you have mentorship, structured training, or rotation opportunities?”
- “For promotion, what weighs more: business outcomes, competency model, or overall impact?”

---

## 8. Extra high-value questions to add

In addition to your six topics, strongly consider:

1. Reporting line and team size
2. Role boundary beyond JD
3. Travel frequency and long-term onsite requirement
4. Performance distribution and forced ranking policy
5. Offer timeline, background check, onboarding checklist
6. Non-compete/confidentiality scope, duration, compensation
7. Leave policy (annual leave, welfare leave, comp-off usability)
8. Probation termination clauses and notice period

---

## 9. Communication framework: polite but precise

Use the 4-step pattern:

1. Appreciation
2. Purpose
3. Question
4. Confirmation recap

Template:

> “Thanks for the detailed introduction today. To make a solid onboarding plan and avoid misunderstandings, may I reconfirm a few points:
> 1) fixed vs variable package structure,
> 2) insurance/fund city and base rule,
> 3) probation duration, pay ratio, and conversion criteria.
> My current understanding is: ... Could you please correct anything inaccurate?”

---

## 10. Post-interview follow-up message template

> Hello, thank you for the discussion today.
> To avoid misunderstanding on my side, may I confirm the following in writing:
> - compensation structure,
> - payroll date and bonus timing,
> - insurance/fund city, base and ratio,
> - probation duration, salary ratio, conversion criteria,
> - schedule and overtime/comp-off policy.
> If any of my notes are inaccurate, please let me know. Thank you very much.

---

## Conclusion

HR communication is not bargaining for its own sake. It is about setting clear, trusted collaboration boundaries.

Do not focus on asking more. Focus on asking:

- with structure,
- with respect,
- with confirmable outcomes (preferably written).

Wish you a transparent, growth-friendly, and fair offer.
