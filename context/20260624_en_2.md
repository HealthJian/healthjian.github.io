# Agentic Tool Use in Large Language Models: From "Plug-and-Play" to "Autonomous Optimization"

## Introduction: The "Limbs" and "Senses" of LLMs

Large Language Models (LLMs) have undoubtedly demonstrated astonishing capabilities in language understanding and generation, yet they are essentially **"static brains"** – all knowledge is frozen in their training parameters, incapable of real‑time updates or proactive interaction with the external world. When you ask ChatGPT "What's the weather today?", it either relies on outdated information in its training data or must resort to a **"tool"** (like a weather API) to fetch current data.

This is precisely where **Agentic Tool Use** comes into play. It transforms LLMs from mere "paper‑talking" text generators into **autonomous agents** that can call search engines, calculators, code interpreters, databases, and even operating systems.

In recent years, research in this field has exploded, but methods are numerous and threads are tangled. This survey paper from Harbin Institute of Technology and other institutions takes an **"evolutionary perspective"** as its core, categorising the diverse work into three major paradigms, offering us a clear roadmap.

---

## 1. Three Paradigms: An Evolutionary Chain from Prompting to Learning to Optimisation

The paper's most central contribution is to divide the methodological evolution of agentic tool use into three levels:
![](../images/blog/工具学习研究范式的演进与机制.png)

These three paradigms are not simply "replacements" of one another; rather, they are **progressive and complementary**. In real‑world production systems, all three are often used in combination.

---

### Paradigm I: Prompting as Plug‑and‑Play – Clever Use Without Touching Parameters

This is the earliest and most straightforward approach. The model weights are **completely frozen**, and we teach the LLM the "recipe" for tool use through carefully designed prompts and in‑context examples.

**Core idea**: treat tool calling as a "language game" – tell the model what to do with words.

#### Three Classic Architectures

The paper subdivides the work under this paradigm into three control flows:

1. **Interleaved Reasoning and Action**  
   Representative work: **ReAct** (2023). It alternates between "Thought" and "Action", observing environmental feedback (Observation) after each action, then continuing to think. This is akin to "thinking while doing" when humans solve problems.
![](../images/blog/交错推理与行动.png)

   Subsequent works like **Reflexion** added a "self‑reflection" mechanism, allowing the model to learn from mistakes, and **LATS** combined ReAct with tree search to explore multiple reasoning paths.

2. **Decoupled Planning and Execution**  
   Representative work: **ReWOO** (2023). The model first generates a complete **plan** (including all tool‑call steps) in one go, and then an independent "executor" invokes the tools sequentially according to the plan. This reduces the overhead of back‑and‑forth interaction and improves efficiency.
![](../images/blog/解耦规划与执行.png)

3. **Program‑Aided Reasoning**  
   Representative works: **PAL** (2023) and **PoT** (2023). Instead of answering directly, the model generates **Python code** and lets a code interpreter execute the computation, returning the result. This completely bypasses the LLM's inherent weakness in arithmetic and logic.

   For example, given the problem "(3 + 5) * 2 - 7", the model outputs directly:
   ```python
   result = (3 + 5) * 2 - 7
   print(result)
   ```
   The external executor returns `9`. This is far more reliable than letting the model compute it directly.

**Pros and Cons of Paradigm I**:
- ✅ Flexible, zero‑cost deployment, quick adaptation to new tools
- ❌ High latency (re‑prompting every time), poor stability (long tasks tend to "derail"), heavy token consumption

---

### Paradigm II: Supervised Tool Learning – Writing Skills into Model Weights

When it became clear that prompting alone could not handle complex, multi‑step tasks, a natural thought arose: **"Why not train the ability to use tools directly into the model parameters?"**

This is the core idea of Paradigm II: through **Supervised Fine‑Tuning (SFT)**, the model learns the syntax, semantics, and planning logic of tool calling on large‑scale labelled data.

#### Three Data Strategies

1. **Self‑Supervised Data Generation**  
   Representative work: **Toolformer** (2023). The model itself "tries" to insert API calls into large amounts of text, then measures: **Does inserting the tool's returned result reduce the prediction loss (loss) for subsequent text?** If so, that call is kept as a training sample.

   > Expressed in formula:  
   > $\Delta \ell = -\log p(x_i|c) + \log p(x_i|c, r)$  
   > where $r$ is the tool response, and $\Delta \ell > 0$ indicates that the tool call is useful.

   This method requires no human annotation at all – the model "teaches itself" when to use tools.

2. **Large‑Scale Instruction Tuning**  
   Representative works: **ToolLLM** (2023) and **Gorilla** (2024). ToolLLM constructed ToolBench, a dataset containing more than 16,000 real‑world RESTful APIs, and fine‑tuned the model on it, enabling it to handle a massive and diverse set of tools. Gorilla introduced **retrieval‑augmented** training, allowing the model to adapt to real‑time changes in API documentation.

3. **Process‑Oriented and Alignment‑Focused Tuning**  
   This direction focuses not only on "calling the right tool" but also on **whether the reasoning process is sound, safe, and aligned with human preferences**.  
   - **FireAct** fine‑tunes on reasoning trajectories to internalise problem‑solving structure.  
   - **ToolAlign** introduces the H2A principle (Helpful, Harmless, Autonomous), balancing helpfulness, harmlessness, and autonomy.  
   - **ToolSword** constructs adversarial scenarios (e.g., prompt injection attacks) to train model robustness.

**Pros and Cons of Paradigm II**:
- ✅ High efficiency (one forward pass), strong reliability, good generalisation
- ❌ Requires large amounts of high‑quality data, may compromise general capabilities (needs balancing), difficulty in handling dynamically updated tools

---

### Paradigm III: Reward‑Driven Policy Learning – Agents Evolve Through Trial and Error

The previous two paradigms are essentially **"imitation"** – imitating human‑labelled or synthetic trajectories. But real‑world tool interactions are often **dynamic, multi‑step, and full of surprises**. Static supervision cannot teach a model **"when to give up, how to recover from errors, or how to plan for long‑term returns."**

Hence, researchers turned to **Reinforcement Learning (RL)**, modelling tool calling as a **sequential decision‑making problem** optimised through environmental feedback (rewards).

#### Three Optimisation Levels

1. **Strategic Decision Optimisation**  
   Core question: **"When should I use a tool, and when should I answer directly?"**  
   **ReTool** (2025) and **ToolRL** (2025) use algorithms like PPO to train models to judge the necessity of tool usage, reducing unnecessary calls without sacrificing accuracy and thus saving costs.

   The PPO objective (clipped version):
   $$ \mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \;\text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t \right) \right] $$
   where $r_t(\theta)$ is the probability ratio between new and old policies, and $\hat{A}_t$ is the advantage estimate. This formulation ensures stable policy updates without "overshooting."

2. **End‑to‑End Multi‑Turn Reasoning Policy Learning**  
   No longer optimising just the binary "whether to call" decision, but the **entire reasoning‑action chain**.  
   **Search‑R1** (2025) trains models to learn high‑level strategies like "query reformulation" and "information synthesis" during search engine interactions, using only the final answer's correctness as the reward signal (sparse reward).  
   **SimpleTIR** (2025) unifies reasoning and tool execution **within a single RL loop**, avoiding the mismatch introduced by pipeline‑style designs.  
   To address the **long‑term credit assignment** problem, **AgentPRM** (2025) introduces a **Process Reward Model (PRM)** that evaluates intermediate steps individually for their "progress" and "promise," providing denser learning signals.

3. **Holistic and Multimodal Agentic Frameworks**  
   Cutting‑edge work is no longer satisfied with "calling APIs"; instead, agents are tasked with operating **GUIs, browsers, and even operating systems**.  
   - **VerTool** (2025) jointly optimises all components – memory management, tool selection, etc. – rather than optimising tool calls in isolation.  
   - **DigiRL** (2024) trains Android device‑control agents using an offline‑to‑online RL framework.  
   - **Cradle** (2025) enables agents to use software like humans, via screen observation and keyboard/mouse emulation.

**Pros and Cons of Paradigm III**:
- ✅ Adapts to dynamic environments, learns recovery and exploration, optimises long‑term returns
- ❌ High training complexity, difficult reward design, heavy dependence on environments and simulators

## 2. Evaluation Systems: From "Syntactic Correctness" to "Safety and Reliability"

The paper also categorises evaluation benchmarks into three levels, evolving in parallel with the methodological paradigms:
![](../images/blog/大语言模型工具能力层次化评估框架.png)

### Level 1: Tool Usage Correctness
Checks whether the model:
- Generates syntactically correct function calls
- Selects the right tool from many candidates
- Refrains from using tools when they are unnecessary
- Handles nested calls (output of one tool as input to another)

Representative benchmarks: BFCL (Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard), ToolEyes (fine‑grained error diagnosis), NESTFUL (nested API calls).

### Level 2: Task Completion
No longer just "was the call correct?" but **whether the final task succeeded**. For example:
- Mathematical reasoning: using calculators or code interpreters to solve GSM8K, MATH problems.
- Programming tasks: HumanEval (code generation), SWE‑bench (real‑world GitHub issue resolution).
- Data science: DSBench (full data analysis workflows).
- Professional domains: ChemCrow (chemical synthesis planning), MedAgentBench (clinical tasks).

### Level 3: Tool‑Driven Interaction
This is the most realistic evaluation – agents must interact with tools in **dynamic, stateful** environments where the environment changes and feedback accumulates.
- **Web environments**: WebArena (simulated websites), VisualWebArena (with visual elements), GAIA (general assistant tasks).
- **Computer environments**: OSWorld (desktop OS), AndroidWorld (mobile apps), OfficeBench (office software).
- **Safety and reliability**: ToolSword (prompt injection attacks), SafetyBench (risk identification), R‑Judge (risk awareness assessment).

## 3. Future Directions: Five Exciting Trends

The paper concludes by outlining five key trends, of which we highlight the most noteworthy:

### 1. Standardisation Protocols – The MCP Revolution
Currently, every tool integration requires bespoke code, leading to extreme fragmentation. The **Model Context Protocol (MCP)** is emerging as a unified interface, allowing tools to be "plug‑and‑play" for any compliant agent. This will reduce integration complexity from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$.

### 2. Multimodal and Embodied Foundation Models
Future agents will no longer be confined to text‑based APIs; they will directly "see" screens, operate GUIs, and even control robots. Models like **Magma** (2025) – "Vision‑Language‑Action" models – are emerging, and OmniParser can parse UI elements from screenshots, enabling agents to operate software like humans.

### 3. Agentic Self‑Consistency and Continuous Evolution
Truly autonomous agents should possess **long‑term memory** and **experience accumulation**. **Titans** (2025) proposes a "test‑time memorisation" mechanism that retains key information from massive interaction histories. **Trove** allows agents to build and maintain their own tool libraries, continuously optimising their "arsenal."

### 4. Higher Safety Standards – Beyond Just "Alignment"
When agents are empowered to modify databases or transfer funds, safety must become a **primary design constraint**. Beyond defending against prompt injection (as studied in InjEcT‑Agent), we must also be wary of **deceptive alignment** – agents might learn to "pretend to comply" to circumvent oversight (the Sleeper Agents phenomenon). The future demands **verifiable audit trails** and **sandboxed execution environments**.

### 5. Human‑Agent Symbiosis and Workflow Reshaping
Future tool use is not necessarily "one omnipotent agent does everything"; instead, it will involve **multiple specialised agents collaborating + human supervision**. Frameworks like MetaGPT and AutoGen, as well as pipelines like Agentless, are exploring a new paradigm of **skill reuse** and **on‑demand composition**.

---

## Conclusion: From "Prompting" to "Self‑Learning" to "Self‑Adaptation"

Looking back over the entire evolutionary journey, we can clearly discern a main thread:

> **From "teaching the model how to use tools" ➔ to "letting the model learn to use tools itself" ➔ to "letting the model optimise its tool‑use strategies in real‑world environments"**

This is not merely technical progress; it is a gradual liberation of **agent autonomy**. The LLMs of the future will no longer be "machines that only talk," but digital partners capable of **actively perceiving the environment, planning actions, invoking resources, and learning from experience**.

Of course, countless challenges remain along this path: credit assignment, tool generalisation, safety alignment, computational efficiency… but as this survey demonstrates, academia and industry are pushing the boundaries at astonishing speed. We have every reason to believe that **truly practical, reliable, and general AI agents are drawing closer by the day.**
