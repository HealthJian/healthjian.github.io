# The Path to Optimizing Large Language Model Agents: From Parameter Fine-Tuning to Parameter-Free Evolution

## Introduction: When LLMs Don the Mantle of "Agents"

Large Language Models (LLMs) are undoubtedly the brightest stars in the AI landscape in recent years. They can write poetry, generate code, and perform reasoning—but have you ever wondered: **if all we do is ask an LLM to answer a static question, what makes it any different from an advanced encyclopedia?**

True intelligence is embodied in the ability to **interact with the environment, make autonomous decisions, and learn continuously**. This is precisely the mission of **LLM-based Agents**: using the LLM as the "brain" and endowing it with capabilities such as perception, planning, memory, action, and execution, so that it can complete complex multi‑step tasks just like a human.

However, ideals are often at odds with reality. Raw LLMs are trained for "next‑token prediction"; they are not inherently good at **long‑term planning, error recovery, or dynamic adaptation**. As a result, researchers have begun to focus on a central question:

> **How can we optimize LLMs to make them more powerful and more reliable agents?**

This survey paper, from East China Normal University and other institutions, is the first to systematically divide all methods in this field into two major camps—**Parameter‑driven** and **Parameter‑free**—and further breaks them down into six optimization strategies. Let us follow this clear roadmap to get a complete picture of LLM agent optimization.

## 1. A Panoramic Overview: The Two Main Threads of Optimization

The entire optimization universe can be divided as follows:
![](../images/blog/llmagent优化.png)

**Parameter‑driven** methods are like "training athletes" – they adjust the internal weights of the model so that the LLM fundamentally learns the behavioural patterns of an agent. **Parameter‑free** methods are like "equipping athletes with gear" – they leave the model itself unchanged, but improve performance at inference time through prompt engineering, external knowledge, multi‑agent collaboration, and similar techniques.

These two approaches are not opposed but **complementary**. In practice, the most powerful agents are often both "well‑trained" and "well‑equipped".

## 2. Parameter‑Driven Optimization: Reshaping the Agent's "Muscle Memory"

### 2.1 Traditional Fine‑Tuning Optimization – Starting with Imitation

This is the most basic optimisation path. The core idea is: **collect a large amount of high‑quality "agent behaviour trajectories" and then teach the LLM these trajectories via supervised learning.**

The entire process consists of two steps:
![](../images/blog/轨迹数据构建与微调流程.png)

#### Where do trajectory data come from?

The paper summarises four main sources, each with its own trade‑offs:

| Source | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| **Expert annotation** | Highest quality, good task alignment | High cost, limited scale, difficult to obtain |
| **Strong LLM generation** (e.g., GPT‑4) | Stable, high‑quality | Expensive, may introduce biases |
| **Self‑exploration in environments** | Low cost, easy to scale | May produce low‑quality trajectories; strict filtering required |
| **Multi‑agent collaborative generation** | Good diversity, robust | Coordination mechanisms are complex and hard to design |

Among these, **self‑exploration** has received significant attention in recent years. For example, **ReAct‑style** trajectories (alternating thought → action → observation) are widely used as basic data. **Toolformer** even lets the model "try" to insert API calls in the corpus by itself—if the insertion reduces prediction loss, the call is kept, all without any human annotation!

#### Data filtering: putting good steel on the blade

Not all generated data should be accepted; they must be filtered. The paper classifies filtering methods into three categories:

- **Environment‑feedback‑based**: binary rewards based on task success/failure (e.g., AgentTuning).
- **Human/rule‑based**: manual inspection or scoring based on predefined criteria (e.g., diversity, consistency).
- **Model‑based**: automatic scoring and ranking using LLMs such as GPT‑4 (e.g., COEVOL).

#### Turning low‑quality data into treasure

Failed trajectories are also valuable. Some methods (e.g., ETO, AgentBank) pair successful and failed trajectories so that the model learns to distinguish "right" from "wrong". Others use failed examples to train the model to recognise errors and avoid hallucinations.

#### The three musketeers of fine‑tuning strategies

- **Standard SFT**: full parameter fine‑tuning, the most direct and effective. AgentTuning, Agent‑FLAN, and others adopt this method.
- **Parameter‑Efficient Fine‑Tuning (PEFT)**: e.g., LoRA, QLoRA, updating only a small number of parameters, greatly reducing resource consumption. Widely used in FireAct, Agent Lumos, etc.
- **Customised fine‑tuning**: designing task‑specific loss functions or training procedures, such as ATM's MITO loss (combining SFT with KL regularisation) and ENVISIONS' contrastive learning with cyclic fine‑tuning.

**Summary**: Traditional fine‑tuning provides a stable and controllable baseline, but its essence is **"cloning"** – imitating existing behaviours, lacking the ability to explore and adapt to new environments. This naturally leads us to the next level.

### 2.2 Reinforcement Learning Optimisation – Evolving Through Trial and Error

If fine‑tuning is "cooking by recipe", then reinforcement learning is "tasting and adjusting the heat yourself". The LLM agent interacts with the environment and optimises its strategy based on **reward signals** or **preference comparisons**.

The paper divides RL optimisation into two branches:

#### Branch 1: Reward‑function‑driven optimisation

The core is to design a reward function that tells the agent "good" or "bad", and then update the policy using algorithms such as PPO and Actor‑Critic.

Reward sources are diverse:

| Reward type | Representative work | Characteristics |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Environment reward** | AgentGym, CMAT | Directly from task success/failure, simple but sparse |
| **Model reward** | WebRL, StepAgent | Fine‑grained, step‑level feedback via auxiliary models |
| **Custom reward** | AGILE, SaySelf, DeepResearcher | Composite rewards combined with task‑specific objectives (e.g., efficiency, confidence) |

For example, **AGILE** designs a reward function that balances task completion with the cost of "asking an expert for help", allowing the agent to proactively seek help when stuck rather than persisting blindly. **DeepResearcher** enables the agent to perform multiple rounds of search and reasoning in real‑world web environments, optimising the entire research process with RL.

#### Branch 2: Preference alignment optimisation

When explicit reward signals are hard to define, preference alignment becomes an alternative. The most popular method is **DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)**.

DPO does not require training a separate reward model; instead, it directly uses preference data $(x, y_w, y_l)$ (where $y_w$ is the preferred response and $y_l$ is the dispreferred one) and optimises via a concise loss function:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{DPO} = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{ref}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{ref}(y_l|x)}\right)\right] $$

The essence of this formula is: **increase the relative probability of the better response and decrease that of the worse one**, while the parameter $\beta$ controls the update magnitude to prevent deviating too far from the reference model.

Preference data can be constructed via different strategies:

- **Expert/human preferences**: directly using human annotations or expert trajectories (e.g., IPR, DMPO)
- **Task/environment preferences**: automatically generating preference pairs based on task success rates or environmental feedback (e.g., ETO, Agent Q)

**Agent Q** is a standout among these: it uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to explore the action space, constructs preference pairs from MCTS visit counts and success feedback, and then fine‑tunes the policy with DPO, achieving significant improvements on complex interactive tasks.

### 2.3 Hybrid Fine‑Tuning Optimisation – Learning from Both

Since SFT is stable but lacks exploration, while RL is strong in exploration but costly, why not **warm up with SFT and then sprint with RL**?

This is the core idea of hybrid optimisation. Most work adopts a **sequential** hybrid: first train a basic agent via behaviour cloning (BC) or SFT, then apply RL (PPO/DPO) on top of it.

For example:
- **ETO**: first BC, then DPO for preference alignment.
- **AGILE**: first SFT, then PPO for decision optimisation.
- **AMOR**: first SFT, then KTO (Kahneman‑Tversky Optimization) for preference tuning.

A few **non‑sequential** methods also exist, such as **OPTIMA**, which alternately applies SFT and DPO during training for iterative improvement.

Hybrid optimisation has become the most popular paradigm today, and OpenAI's **Reinforcement Fine‑Tuning (RFT)** is a typical example of this approach.

## 3. Parameter‑Free Optimisation: Getting Stronger Without Changing the Model

Not all optimisation requires modifying model parameters. In resource‑constrained or fast‑iteration scenarios, parameter‑free methods provide lightweight and flexible alternatives.

### 3.1 Experience‑Driven Optimisation – Giving Agents a "Memory"

These methods store historical interaction experiences in **memory modules** and retrieve/reuse them in subsequent tasks.

- **Optimus‑1** transforms exploration trajectories into hierarchical knowledge graphs to assist task planning.
- **Agent Hospital** maintains a medical record library and an experience base, continuously updating guidance rules based on successful and failed cases.
- **Expel** and **AutoManual** let agents autonomously collect, organise, and retrieve experiential knowledge to guide future actions.

This is analogous to how humans learn from their own experiences – the more you do, the more of a veteran you become.

---

### 3.2 Feedback‑Driven Optimisation – Growing from Criticism

Feedback can come from the environment or from the model's own evaluations. It is divided into three sub‑categories:

1. **Self‑Reflection**  
   **Reflexion** converts task results or heuristic evaluations into textual feedback, allowing the model to reflect on itself and adjust its behaviour. **SAGE** introduces an "inspector" agent that provides iterative feedback, while the "assistant" agent self‑corrects based on that feedback.

2. **External Feedback**  
   Similar to Actor‑Critic architectures, an external model or module evaluates the agent's actions.  
   **Retroformer** trains a "retrospection model" to analyse failure causes; **COPPER** uses a shared "reflector" to generate counterfactual feedback; **InteRecAgent** uses a "critic LLM" to check whether the "actor" violates rules.

3. **Meta‑Prompt Optimisation**  
   Instead of modifying model parameters, this approach **optimises the prompts themselves**.  
   **OPRO** extracts information from failed attempts and iteratively generates better instructions; **MetaReflection** is similar; **MPO** trains a "meta‑planner" via SFT and DPO to guide high‑level prompt design.

---

### 3.3 Tool‑Driven Optimisation – Learning to "Delegate" to External Resources

One of the key differences between agents and plain LLMs is the ability to **call external tools** (search engines, calculators, code interpreters, etc.). Tool optimisation methods aim to make agents smarter in selecting and using tools.

- **TPTU** optimises task decomposition and tool invocation order.
- **AVATAR** diagnoses tool usage issues by comparing performance differences across sample pairs.
- **Middleware** introduces error‑feedback mechanisms to align tool inputs and outputs.
- **AgentOptimizer** treats functions as learnable weights and iteratively adjusts the tool set based on execution results.

---

### 3.4 Retrieval‑Driven Optimisation – External Knowledge as an Auxiliary Brain

Retrieval‑augmented (RAG) methods enable agents to dynamically fetch external knowledge during inference, improving factual accuracy and adaptability.

- **Self‑RAG** combines retrieval with self‑reflection to iteratively refine generated content.
- **RaDA** leverages dynamic retrieval and past experience for task decomposition and action generation.
- **WebThinker** implements a deep research loop of "reasoning → retrieval → synthesis", enhanced with RL for tool invocation.
- **FlowSearch** constructs a dynamic multi‑agent knowledge‑flow framework that supports parallel exploration and recursive evidence integration.

---

### 3.5 Multi‑Agent Collaborative Optimisation – Many Hands Make Light Work

Having multiple agents, each with a distinct role, work together often yields intelligence that surpasses that of a single agent.

- **MetaGPT** and **ChatDev** role‑play the software development process (planning, coding, debugging, documentation).
- **DyLAN** and **MacNet** dynamically construct agent networks, using importance scoring and early‑stopping signals to optimise reasoning.
- **Agentverse** and **CAPO** support iterative proposal, evaluation, and plan revision.
- **SMoA** and **MAD** coordinate multi‑agent debates through "judges" or "selectors" to improve decision quality.

These frameworks demonstrate that **collaborative structure itself is a powerful optimisation mechanism**.

## 4. Evaluation and Datasets: How Do We Measure Progress?

Without measurement, there is no optimisation. The paper systematically reviews evaluation methodologies and commonly used datasets.

### Evaluation Methodologies

- **Human evaluation**: suitable for subjective tasks (creativity, coherence, emotional intelligence, etc.), but costly and hard to scale.
- **Automated evaluation**:
  - **Static datasets**: using established metrics (accuracy, execution success rate) for objective tasks.
  - **LLM as judge**: employing more powerful models to score or compare outputs.
  - **Interactive environment evaluation**: running agents in simulators and combining rule‑based checks, execution feedback, and model judgments.

### Commonly Used Benchmark Datasets

| Task Domain | Representative Datasets | Evaluation Metrics |
|-------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Mathematical reasoning | GSM8K, MATH, AIME | Exact Match, Pass@k |
| Question answering | HotpotQA, MMLU, PubMedQA | EM, F1, Accuracy |
| Code generation | HumanEval, SWE‑bench, LiveCodeBench | Pass@k, execution success rate |
| Tool invocation | T‑Eval, ToolEval, MINT‑Bench | selection accuracy, call success rate |
| Web navigation | WebArena, Mind2Web, WebShop | task success rate, step efficiency |
| Environment interaction | ALFWorld, ScienceWorld, RLCard | success rate, average reward |
| Multimodal | VQA v2.0, A‑OKVQA, EgoSchema | VQA accuracy, reasoning accuracy |

In addition, there are **multi‑task comprehensive benchmarks** such as AgentBench, AgentEval, and GAIA, which cover multiple domains and are used to evaluate the generalisation ability of agents.

### Fine‑Tuning‑Specific Datasets

To teach LLMs agent behaviours, specialised trajectory datasets are indispensable. Examples include AgentInstruct, AgentBank, Agent‑FLAN, FireAct, ToRA‑CORPUS, etc., which contain high‑quality ReAct‑style trajectories from web, programming, mathematics, tool invocation, and other scenarios.

## 5. Real‑World Applications: Agents Are Changing These Fields

### 🏥 Healthcare

Agents are already used in medical Q&A, diagnosis, treatment planning, and clinical simulation. **AI Hospital** provides a dynamic doctor‑patient interaction environment; **MDAgents** simulates collaboration between general practitioners and specialists. Recent methods (e.g., HuatuoGPT‑o1) combine PPO/DPO to reinforce long‑term clinical reasoning. However, medical hallucinations, privacy compliance, and long‑record processing remain challenges.

### 🔬 Scientific Research

**CellAgent** automates single‑cell data analysis; **ProtAgents** assists in protein design; **CRISPR‑GPT** designs gene‑editing experiments. **AlphaEvolve** lets code‑evolving agents autonomously discover efficient algorithms; **InternAgent** builds a closed‑loop system from idea generation to experiment execution. The high computational cost and long knowledge‑retrieval cycles in science remain to be overcome.

### 🦾 Embodied AI

In robotics, **Voyager** demonstrates long‑term memory and iterative skill learning; **SayCan** combines LLM reasoning with real‑robot actions. Multimodal methods such as **AutoT** and **Multiply** integrate visual, linguistic, and haptic signals. Uncertainty in the real world, safety, and long‑horizon consistency are the biggest challenges.

### 💰 Finance

Financial agents handle market prediction, trading decisions, and risk management. **FinMem** and **FinCon** use hierarchical memory and concept reinforcement; **TradingAgents** adopts multi‑agent analyst collaboration. **FinVerse** introduces code‑execution capability for precise computation. Data sensitivity, rapid market changes, and high explainability requirements are key issues.

## 6. Challenges and Future Directions

### 6.1 Algorithmic Adaptability and Efficiency

Current RL algorithms (e.g., PPO) are computationally heavy, while DPO, though lightweight, is better suited for single‑step optimisation. **Hybrid methods** are a promising direction to improve adaptability and efficiency. Furthermore, distribution shift during domain transfer is severe, requiring better generalisation strategies.

### 6.2 Standardised Evaluation Metrics

Different tasks use different metrics, making cross‑comparison difficult. There is a need for unified benchmarks that not only measure task completion but also **quantify the "delta" of optimisation**—that is, track step‑wise improvement.

### 6.3 Cost and Efficiency Constraints

Long contexts and multi‑turn interactions lead to explosive token consumption and latency. Solutions include **context optimisation**, **adaptive memory**, **efficient prompt engineering**, and training environments that can be automatically generated or deployed locally.

### 6.4 Safety and Robustness

Agents are vulnerable to prompt injection and jailbreaking attacks, and they behave unstably in dynamic environments. Defence strategies include input/output filtering, multi‑model consensus, human‑in‑the‑loop verification, and **alignment‑driven training** with **safety‑aware optimisation**.

### 6.5 Multi‑Agent System Optimisation

Most current multi‑agent systems rely on frozen LLMs and prompt‑based workflows, lacking **joint parameter optimisation**. Future research needs to explore mechanisms such as role specification, information exchange, reward sharing, and hierarchical decision‑making, so that multi‑agent systems can truly co‑evolve.

---

## Conclusion: Optimisation Is the Engine of Agent Evolution

From "behaviour‑cloning" fine‑tuning, to "trial‑and‑error" reinforcement learning, to "silent" prompt and retrieval enhancement—the optimisation path of LLM agents is rapidly evolving along a trajectory **from static to dynamic, from single to collaborative, from imitation to innovation**.

As this survey reveals, **no single method can cover all scenarios**. The agent systems of the future will inevitably be "hybrids" that fuse multiple strategies: SFT to build a solid foundation, RL to hone decision‑making, prompts and retrieval to adapt to change, and multi‑agent collaboration to break through limitations.

And we stand at the forefront of this evolutionary wave, witnessing the transformation of artificial intelligence from "a model that talks" to "a partner that acts".
